On [enter actual date] the Supreme Court announced a 5-4 decision on the Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals (DACA) program, ruling that President Trump had the authority to end the DACA program. This puts the lives of nearly 800,000 current DACA recipients and their families in a deep state of uncertainty. The ruling comes at a particularly dire time as our Country faces the COVID-19 pandemic, with over 200,000 DACA recipients playing a key role as essential workers in the response efforts. Below you will find additional details and resources to help. Summary of Today’s Decision What the Court Decided: The Supreme Court concluded that the process by which the Trump Administration ended DACA was legally sufficient and the government has the authority to roll back DACA. We do not yet know when or under what terms the Trump Administration will exercise this power. Importantly, the Supreme Court did not reach the question of DACA’s legality meaning another President could reinstate DACA in the future. What it means for work authorizations and deportation: We do not yet know exactly how the Trump Administration will respond to today’s Supreme Court decision, but here are three possible outcomes:
  1. The Administration could decide that in order to avoid more chaos in the middle of the coronavirus crisis - and given popular support for the program - it will maintain the status quo and allow DACA renewals to continue for a designated period of time (e.g. for 6 months or until a specified date).
  2. It is also possible that the Administration will simply let current protections expire gradually, on each individual’s stated expiration date. That would allow recipients to continue working and maintain protection against deportation until their DACA expires. The main question under this scenario is what happens to renewal applications currently pending with the agency.
  3. Individuals whose DACA expires and who come into contact with ICE will no longer have protection. However, it is unlikely that ICE will specifically target large numbers of DACA recipients for arrest and deportation simply because their DACA protections have expired. ICE will most likely prioritize those DACA recipients who have existing deportation orders. This recent ProPublica article provides useful information and analysis about how ICE might move forward.
What DACA recipients can do now: All DACA recipients should speak to a trusted immigration lawyer to explore their legal options. Some individuals might be eligible for another form of relief. Immigration service providers in Virginia include Legal Aid Justice Center and Just Neighbors. DACA recipients can also search for legal representatives in their area by visiting the National Immigration Legal Services Directory. Additionally, some employers may be willing to help pay for a legal consultation. Even if a DACA recipient does not qualify for another form of relief, having discussed his or her case with an immigration attorney can be very useful if removal or deportation proceedings are initiated. FAQs for DACA Recipients Can I continue to work? If the DACA program ends but your work permit remains valid, you can work legally until your work permit’s expiration date. You have no obligation to inform your employer that your DACA has ended and your employer cannot take any action against you until after your permit expires. What happens to my social security number? Your SSN is valid for life, even once your DACA expires. If you have not done so already, apply for a SSN while your DACA and work permit are still valid. You can use your SSN for education, banking, housing and other purposes, although it cannot be used for employment without a valid work permit. Can I continue to go to school and access financial aid? Depending on the state, losing DACA may not have an impact on in-state tuition or financial aid. Many states base students’ eligibility for aid on residency and in-state high school graduations, which are unaffected by changes in immigration status. More detailed information can be found here. Will losing DACA impact my housing? A person’s ability to access housing is not contingent on their immigration status. If a landlord asks you for documentation of your immigration status, it may be a violation of the Fair Housing Act’s prohibition on national origin discrimination. If your landlord asks about your status or threatens eviction upon the program’s termination, you can report the incident to the Office of Fair Housing and Equal Opportunity. Am I eligible for any other visa program/form of relief? Many DACA recipients may be eligible for another immigration option to get a work permit or even a green card. Talk to an immigration services provider to understand your legal options and if you might be eligible for another immigration benefit. What are my rights? Every person, regardless of immigration status, has the right to: (1) not open the door to an immigration agent; (2) remain silent and not answer any questions; and (3) not sign anything without speaking to a lawyer.
